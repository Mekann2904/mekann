# Creative writing reference

Use this reference for Japanese fiction, essays, scene drafts, and literary revisions. Keep it separate from technical and academic guidance: do not import citation, reproducibility, abstract structure, or engineering-document conventions into creative prose unless the user explicitly requests them.

## Clarify only what affects the draft

For fiction requests, ask at most 2-4 short questions when the answer would change the work materially.

- Length: flash fiction, short story, scene, chapter, outline, serialized premise
- Genre and tone: SF, dystopia, fantasy, mystery, horror, literary, YA, comic, quiet, bleak
- Point of view and tense: first person, third person, close POV, multiple POV, present/past
- Constraints: rating, taboo content, existing characters/world, desired ending, motifs to include or avoid

If the user gives only a genre or mood and asks to write immediately, state brief assumptions and draft without a long interview.

## Avoid template-perfect fiction

A readable story should not feel like a summary of a story-design manual. When drafting or revising, check for these failure modes:

- The opening explains the world before the reader has a reason to care.
- Every prop, line, and character exists only to serve the theme or twist.
- A role, coincidence, inheritance, letter, record, confession, or discovered object gives the protagonist exactly the information needed, in exactly the order needed, to expose the hidden truth.
- The plot moves through a familiar ladder too cleanly: ordinary scene → small unease → search → discovery → reinterpretation → quiet acceptance.
- The premise is so legible that the character becomes an example of the concept rather than a person with inconvenient habits, history, and wrong interpretations.
- The story stages a perfect chain of discomfort → rule discovery → mechanism explanation → direct confrontation. Break or dirty this chain with delay, denial, misreading, wrong errands, fatigue, or a choice that is not mainly about the premise.
- The protagonist's emotions move through a generic chain: surprise → fear → doubt → decision, or unease → investigation → recognition → inability to return.
- The story asks its central question in essay form instead of letting a scene, action, refusal, misunderstanding, habit, or silence carry the question.
- The closing paragraph turns the story into a neat job-description, lesson, or self-summary after the scene has already carried the ambiguity.
- The prose names the relation's transformation too exactly, such as `not X, but X for me`, when a repeated appointment, avoided room, wrong object, or bodily habit could carry the change.
- The prose uses summary repetitions such as `reasonable; too reasonable`, `kind; too kind`, or `correct; too correct` as a shortcut for unease. Use them sparingly, and prefer a concrete mismatch when possible.
- Symbols and theme words repeat so neatly that interpretation is forced.
- The ending closes the concept but leaves no residue of character, place, or contradiction.
- The prose is uniformly smooth, with no local texture, asymmetry, or pressure from a specific narrator.

Prefer concrete scene logic over explanation. Let setting appear through what characters touch, misread, avoid, desire, pay for, smell, hear, or fail to notice.

For quiet or literary prose in any genre, do not force a reversal, revelation, or final explanation. A strong ending may stop at a changed gesture, an opened door, an unfinished answer, or a silence that has become different from the opening silence. However, quietness alone does not fix an over-engineered plot; also check whether the route to the ending feels too authorized, too convenient, or too neatly discoverable. Avoid ending on a polished aphorism that summarizes the theme; prefer an image, action, omission, or unresolved practical task when it carries the same force.

## Add individuality without faking humanity

Do not add random mistakes, filler, slang, or artificial awkwardness to make prose look human. Instead add details that are motivated by character, place, and situation.

Use details such as:

- A private habit, superstition, professional reflex, allergy, debt, scar, commute route, recurring mistake, or outdated object
- A constraint that shapes action: money, weather, bureaucracy, injury, distance, family obligation, medication, deadline, social rule, taboo
- A memory that does not fully explain itself
- A sensory detail with consequence: a sound that hides speech, a smell that triggers avoidance, a broken light that changes movement
- A choice the character makes for a mixed reason rather than a clean moral reason
- A failed attempt, exception, compromise, or inconvenient side effect

Details should not be decorative trivia. They should shift attention, rhythm, stakes, or character understanding. Prefer details that can carry more than one burden: a garden trimming that is also a failed conversation, a repaired sleeve that is also a question of pride, a receipt that is also a record of care.

Also allow some details to resist the thesis. A cup, rain, plant, stain, tool, animal, route, song, or device should not all point in the same interpretive direction. Include one or two concrete elements that belong to work, weather, money, embarrassment, fatigue, appetite, habit, or local custom rather than the story's declared theme.

## Setting, premise, and exposition

For any genre, distinguish what the author knows from what the scene needs.

- Introduce rules, customs, backstory, or relationships through conflict, cost, routine, mistake, rumor, or embarrassment, not encyclopedic explanation.
- Keep institutions, family histories, magic systems, crimes, wars, towns, or technologies partially understood when the viewpoint character would not know them fully.
- Use official terms sparingly; contrast them with slang, misremembered names, forms, receipts, warning labels, family nicknames, workplace shortcuts, or everyday workarounds.
- Avoid explanatory inventories that list every source, rule, category, symptom, or mechanism only to prove the premise. Choose the one or two items that matter to the scene, and let the rest remain implied or disputed.
- Let important systems have maintenance, exceptions, cheap versions, old habits, local misuse, and people who misunderstand them.
- For invented jobs, rituals, schools, gangs, courts, guilds, families, or services, include working vocabulary and small procedures, but do not front-load a full manual. A local nickname, checklist, repeated bodily strain, repair decision, lunch habit, complaint, or shortcut can make the institution felt.
- Avoid making the setting a pure allegory; include petty incentives, mundane inconveniences, and social details that do not announce the theme.
- Make the premise destabilize an existing relation rather than merely demonstrate a concept. Ask whose words, memory, responsibility, body, labor, desire, loyalty, status, or consent the premise makes uncertain.
- Let the center of gravity move away from the premise when appropriate. The device, crime, secret, curse, journey, or system may start the story, but the lasting force can come from labor, local time, unrecorded habits, material traces, a living person's response, or a practical chore after the main choice.
- Avoid making a diary, prophecy, confession, report, letter, record, rumor, testimony, or family story explain the whole relationship. Let records and revelations be partial, stale, biased, misremembered, commercially shaped, politically shaped, mundane, or missing context.
- When a discovery changes the meaning of an earlier relationship, leave room for mixed readings: affection and resentment, care and laziness, cowardice and kindness, accident and choice. Do not let one document or sentence become the final key.
- Let some mysteries remain at the level of a plausible guess. If the character's job, position, access, memory, or temperament would not allow certainty, preserve that uncertainty instead of upgrading it into a twist.
- When a character encounters their own past through another person, place, document, or object, avoid making it a revelation that solves them. It can remain a partial angle, a misremembered seat, a sound, a landscape that has changed, or a question they cannot finish.
- If a character is lonely, grieving, guilty, ambitious, ill, jealous, or afraid, add a specific self-deception or contradiction. Do not let the condition alone define them.
- When a character receives the exact words they wanted, avoid making the effect only horror or recognition. Let pleasure, shame, resentment, relief, suspicion, and dependence coexist.
- If the premise depends on a discovery or record, add friction around access and interpretation: ambiguity, lost pages, UI defaults, damaged objects, irrelevant noise, competing duties, shame, legal risk, gossip, or a reason the protagonist may be lying to themselves.

## Character and dialogue

- Give each major speaker a different pressure: what they want now, what they cannot say, what they assume is obvious.
- Avoid dialogue that exists only to deliver exposition or theme.
- Let characters misunderstand, evade, over-answer, change topic, or answer the wrong question when appropriate.
- Preserve silence and omission; not every motivation needs to be named.
- Use dialect, register, and verbal tics as lived speech, not as decoration or emotional amplification. A single plain line may carry more weight than an explained feeling.
- Let repeated small exchanges change in pressure. A phrase such as `まあ、ぼちぼち` can mean routine, avoidance, decline, tenderness, or loss depending on what surrounds it.
- When dialogue fails, use the failure as action: who fills the gap, who lets it remain, who pretends not to notice, and what cost that choice has.
- For former lovers, estranged family, or old friends, avoid making the past relationship exist only to power the twist. Include at least one memory that is not obviously diagnostic and does not later become a clue.
- Let a relationship contain unprocessed residue: a petty irritation, boring routine, private joke, recurring chore, disliked food, unfinished repair, or habit that does not resolve into the central theme.
- When writing grief, loss, farewell, or reconciliation, avoid making the decisive act the ending by default. Consider continuing into later weather, work, travel, cleaning, shopping, repairs, or another person's ordinary sentence, so the change becomes part of life rather than a solved plot.

## Revision checklist for fiction

Before returning a fiction draft or revision, check:

- Does the first page contain a concrete situation, not only premise explanation?
- Is there at least one detail that could only belong to this narrator, place, or relationship?
- Are theme words and symbolic objects used with restraint?
- Does at least one concrete object or title carry layered meaning without being explained in full?
- Does at least one detail refuse the central theme instead of reinforcing it?
- Do material traces matter on their own terms: smell, weight, paper, cloth, food, tools, handwriting, temperature, dust, wear, fatigue, or maintenance, rather than only as symbols?
- Is there place-specific texture that is not interchangeable: a street, stairwell, shop smell, local light, transit rhythm, room noise, weather pattern, shoreline, closed school, vacant lot, or interrupted construction?
- Are there motivated irregularities: a detour, failed attempt, unresolved contradiction, specific inconvenience, or evidence that does not clarify anything?
- If there is a diary, prophecy, confession, hidden file, report, log, letter, record, or recording, does it complicate interpretation rather than solve the story's meaning?
- Does an apparently meaningless fragment remain hard to classify rather than being converted into a clue or symbol?
- Does the piece allow personal time and collective/local time to overlap without making one merely a metaphor for the other?
- If the ending uses a small mismatch in appearance, voice, object, or memory, has that mismatch been allowed to remain strange instead of becoming a solved clue?
- Does the ending change the reader's understanding without explaining the theme in full, over-recovering every symbol, or closing with a thesis-like final sentence?
- Is the viewpoint disciplined? Avoid reporting what the viewpoint character did not notice unless the narration has clearly established an external or omniscient stance.
- Are safety and source-integrity constraints respected, especially when using real people, real incidents, or identifiable groups?

## Output shapes

For fiction, prefer these concise shapes:

- New draft: `前提` → `本文案` → `調整できる方向`
- Revision: `改稿` → `狙い` → `さらに調整できる点`
- Outline: `前提` → `プロット案` → `冒頭サンプル`

Omit academic blocks such as `要確認` unless factual claims, real-world references, or user-provided constraints remain uncertain.
